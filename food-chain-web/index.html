Introduction to Food Chain and Web

A food chain refers to the order of events in an ecosystem, where one living organism eats another organism, and later that organism is consumed by another larger organism. The flow of nutrients and energy from one organism to another at different trophic levels forms a food chain.

Charles Elton introduced the modern concept of the food chains in his 1927 book Animal Ecology. Also, he introduced the idea of food web, which he called food cycle.

Learning Objectives

Goals

  • Differentiate food chains from food webs
  • Define producers and consumers
  • Discuss the Pyramid of Energy and Biomass

Major Components

Sun

Sunlight provides necessary light and energy to plants and other producers in the food chain and food web.

Producers

Producers absorb the sun's radiation and convert it into energy through photosynthesis.

Examples

  • Plants (grass, trees, flowers)
  • Algae (phytoplankton)
  • Cyanobacteria

Consumers

Control overpopulation and provide energy to other organisms.

Examples

  • Rabbit (herbivore)
  • Lion (carnivore)
  • Bear (omnivore)

Decomposers

Help recycle nutrients back into the system by breaking down dead organic matter into smaller molecules that can feed other organisms.

Food Chain vs Food Web

Food Chain

Shows the feeding relationships between organisms.

Example Chain

Sun → Grass → Caterpillar → Squirrel → Eagle

Types

  • Detritus food chain
  • Grazing food chain

Food Web

Consists of several interconnected food chains in a single ecosystem.

Example Web

  • Grass → Grasshopper → Bird → Fox
  • Grass → Grasshopper → Squirrel → Eagle
  • Bird → Eagle

Food webs provide a more accurate representation of energy flow by showing multiple interactions between organisms.

Ecological Pyramids

Energy Pyramid

Shows the flow of energy through an ecosystem at different trophic levels.

Energy Flow

  • Only about 10% of energy transfers between trophic levels
  • 90% is lost as heat or unused energy
  • Energy decreases as you move up the pyramid
  • Energy cannot be recycled in food chains

Trophic Levels

  • Primary Producers (Plants) - 100% energy from sun
  • Primary Consumers - 10% of producer energy
  • Secondary Consumers - 1% of producer energy
  • Tertiary Consumers - 0.1% of producer energy

In a healthy ecosystem, there's more energy at the bottom of the pyramid than at the top, supporting larger populations at lower levels.

Biomass Pyramid

Shows the relative amount of biomass in each trophic level.

Typical Distribution (Terrestrial)

  • Producers: 100kg (Trees, grass, plants)
  • Primary Consumers: 100kg (Herbivores)
  • Secondary Consumers: 10kg (Carnivores)
  • Tertiary Consumers: 1kg (Top predators)

Characteristics

  • Biomass can be measured in fresh or dry weight
  • Shows standing crop at each level
  • Can be inverted in aquatic ecosystems
  • Affected by organism size and numbers

Pyramid Comparison

Energy Pyramid

  • Always upright (never inverted)
  • Shows energy flow and loss
  • Measured in energy units (kcal/m²/yr)
  • Represents productivity

Biomass Pyramid

  • Can be inverted in some ecosystems
  • Shows standing crop biomass
  • Measured in mass per unit area
  • Represents accumulated organic matter

Both pyramids reflect the decrease in available resources at each trophic level, but they measure different aspects of the ecosystem.

Key Points Summary

  • Food Chain - Shows feeding relationships between organisms
  • Food Web - Composed of several interconnected food chains
  • Energy & Biomass Pyramid - Represents the flow and distribution in ecosystems
