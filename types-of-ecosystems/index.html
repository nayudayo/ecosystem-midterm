Introduction to Ecosystems

Comes from the Greek word 'Oikos' meaning home and 'systema' meaning system. It is a geographic area where plants, animals, and other organisms, as well as weather and landscapes, work together to form a bubble of life.

The term 'Ecosystem' was first coined by Arthur George Tansley, an English botanist, in 1935.

Scale

An ecosystem can be as small as an oasis in a desert, or as big as an ocean, spanning thousands of miles.

Biome Pyramid

The 'Biome Pyramid' illustrates how temperature and moisture influence the distribution of Earth's biomes. At the base, warm and wet conditions near the equator support tropical rainforests. As moisture decreases, these transition into savannas and then deserts.

Distribution Pattern

  • Base: Tropical rainforests (warm and wet)
  • Middle: Temperate regions (forests and grasslands)
  • Upper: Boreal forests (taiga)
  • Peak: Tundra (coldest and driest)

Terrestrial Ecosystems

A terrestrial ecosystem is a land-based community of organisms and the interactions of biotic and abiotic components in a given area.

Terrestrial ecosystems cover approximately 140 to 150 million kmÂ², which is about 25 to 30 percent of the total earth surface area.

Forest Ecosystems

Forest Layers

Forest Floor (Ground Level)

Covered with mosses, fungi, and foliage, providing food and shelter for insects, reptiles, and small mammals.

Herbaceous Layer (up to 1.5m)

Consists of grasses and ferns, offering food and protection for insects.

Shrub Layer (up to 4.88m)

Provides shelter for deer and birds, with thorny plants protecting birds from predators.

Tree Layer (5m+)

Home to birds and climbing mammals like squirrels, offering nesting sites and protection.

Forest Types

Temperate Forests
  • Located between 25-50 degrees latitude
  • Four distinct seasons
  • Deciduous trees dominate
  • Vegetation includes maple, walnut, birch
  • Rich in wildlife: deer, bears, squirrels, foxes
Tropical Forests
  • Dense, evergreen forests
  • High rainfall
  • Support over half of Earth's species
  • Found in Amazon, Congo, Southeast Asia
  • Philippine species: Philippine Eagle, Tarsier

Tropical Forest Structure

Emergent Layer

Highest layer where tall trees rise above the canopy, home to birds, bats, and insects.

Canopy Layer

Dense layer of overlapping branches housing most rainforest wildlife, including monkeys and parrots.

Understory Layer

Dimly lit area beneath the canopy, filled with shrubs, small trees, and animals like jaguars and snakes.

Forest Floor

Darkest layer with decomposing plants, fungi, and animals like tigers and tapirs.

Dry Forests

Characteristics
  • Make up nearly half of tropical/subtropical forests
  • Support millions through timber products
  • Important for biodiversity conservation
Threats
  • Population pressure
  • Land demands
  • Deforestation

Home to unique species like the Visayan Warty Pig and Philippine Forest Turtle.

Grassland Ecosystems

Types of Grasslands

Temperate Grasslands
  • Moderate climates
  • Hot summers and cold winters
  • Few trees
Savannas
  • Tropical grasslands
  • Warm year-round
  • Scattered trees
  • Seasonal rainfall

Wildlife

  • Herbivores: Bison, Antelope, Water Buffalo, Zebras
  • Predators: Lions, Cheetahs

Human impact has led to conversion of grasslands into agricultural fields, affecting species like monarch butterflies.

Tundra Ecosystems

Tundra is the coldest of all the biomes. Tundra comes from the Finnish word 'tunturi', meaning 'treeless plain'. It is noted for its frost-molded landscapes, extremely low temperatures, little precipitation, poor nutrients, and short growing seasons.

Types of Tundra

Alpine Tundra
  • Located on high-altitude mountains
  • Trees cannot grow here
  • Growing season: ~180 days
Arctic Tundra
  • Encircles the North Pole
  • Extends to the taiga
  • Winter temperatures average -34Â°C
  • Desert-like conditions

Ecosystem Features

Nutrients

  • Major nutrients: Nitrogen and Phosphorus
  • Nitrogen created by biological fixation
  • Phosphorus created by precipitation
Wildlife

  • Arctic Fox
  • Brown Bear

  • Snowy Owls

Marine Ecosystems

Characteristics

Coverage
  • More than 70% of Earth's surface
  • 97% of Earth's water supply
  • 90% of habitable space
Properties
  • High salt content
  • Average salinity: 35 parts per thousand
  • Largest of Earth's aquatic ecosystems

Freshwater Ecosystems

Classification Factors

  • Temperature
  • Light penetration
  • Nutrients
  • Vegetation

Types

Lentic Ecosystem

Includes biotic plants, animals, and micro-organisms, as well as abiotic physical and chemical interactions.

  • Lakes
  • Ponds
  • Wetlands
Lotic Ecosystem

River ecosystems with flowing waters that drain the landscape, part of larger watershed networks.

Wetlands

Semi-aquatic ecosystems where groundcovers are flooded either permanently or seasonally.

Known for biological diversity, habitat provision, and water purification.

Ecological Processes

Ecological Succession

A gradual shift in species composition, with some populations increasing while others decline, leading to a stable climax community.

Key Aspects

Pioneer Species

First organisms to colonize a disturbed area, modifying the environment for later species.

Species Replacement

New species establish as conditions change due to pioneer species.

Community Structure

Alterations in diversity, biomass, and layering reflect environmental changes.

Climax Community

Stable end-stage community with high species diversity.

Competition and facilitation among species drive succession through dominance shifts.

Population Dynamics

Changes in population size, density, and composition over time due to birth rates, death rates, immigration, and emigration.

Key Aspects

Demographic Changes
  • Birth rates
  • Death rates
  • Age structure changes
Environmental Response
  • Range shifts to favorable environments
  • Phenological changes in life events
  • Adaptation and evolution

Human Impact and Climate Change

Human activities such as deforestation, pollution, and greenhouse gas emissions are significantly altering ecosystems worldwide.

Key Impacts

Population Effects
  • Migration due to rising sea levels
  • Agricultural adaptation needs
  • Infrastructure development requirements
Adaptation Strategies
  • Community planning and preparedness
  • Technological solutions
  • Renewable energy adoption

Conservation Needs

Conservation efforts and sustainable practices are essential to preserve biodiversity in the face of human impact and climate change.
