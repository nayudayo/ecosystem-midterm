Population

A group of individuals of a single species inhabiting an area with potential to interbreed.

Key Word: DYNAMIC

Benefits of Studying Population

  • Saving endangered species
  • Controlling pest populations
  • Managing fish and game population

Demography

Statistical study of population and a set of mathematical tools designed to describe populations and investigate how they change.

Characteristics of Population

Size

Total number of individuals in a population. Can affect potential for adaptation because it affects the amount of genetic variation present in the population.

Density

Number of individuals per unit area. Can affect interactions within a population such as competition for food and the ability to find mates.

Distribution

The geographic area where a particular population of a species occurs.

Dispersion

The spatial relationship between members of a population within a habitat at a particular point in time.

Age Structure

The distribution of ages within a population, which can affect population growth and survival.

Population Density Examples:

  • Densely Distributed: Ants
  • Sparsely Distributed: Tigers

Sampling Techniques

While counting all individuals is most accurate, it's often not feasible. Scientists use sampling techniques to study representative portions of habitats.

Quadrat Method

Used for: Immobile organisms (plants) or very small and slow-moving organisms

Involves marking off square areas within a habitat using sticks, string, or frames. Researchers count individuals within these boundaries at random locations to estimate total population.

Mark and Recapture

Used for: Mobile organisms

Animals are captured, marked (using tags, bands, paint, etc.), released, and later recaptured. The ratio of marked to unmarked individuals in the second sample helps estimate total population size.

Camera Traps

Used for: Rare organisms, especially top predators

  • Equipped with motion sensors
  • Records accurate data without disturbing animals
  • Less invasive than live trap and release

Population Distribution and Range

Habitat Generalists

Species whose populations spread continuously across landscapes.

Example: American Robins can breed in parks, urban areas, farms, and forest edges.

Habitat Specialists

Species that only occur in specific ecosystem types.

Example: Aquatic species in isolated populations bounded by water bodies.

Types of Distribution Patterns

Random Distribution

Individuals are scattered randomly throughout an area.

Example: Dandelions and other plants with wind-dispersed seeds.

Clumped Distribution

Individuals are grouped together in clusters.

Examples:

  • Oak trees (seeds drop nearby)
  • Social animals (schools of fish, herds)

Uniform Distribution

Individuals are spaced evenly throughout an area.

Examples:

  • Sage plants (release growth-inhibiting chemicals)
  • Territorial penguins (maintain nesting territories)
