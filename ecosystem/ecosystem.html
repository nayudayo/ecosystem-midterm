Introduction to Biodiversity

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life on Earth at all its levels, from genes to ecosystems, and encompasses the evolutionary, ecological, and cultural processes that sustain life.

Etymology:

  • Greek word "bios" = life
  • Latin word "diversitas" = variety or difference
  • Term coined by Walter G. Rosen in 1986

Learning Objectives

  1. Define what is biodiversity
  2. Identify the three (3) components of biodiversity

Biology - The Foundation

Biology is the study of all living organisms (plants, animals, microorganisms) and how they interact with each other and their environment.

Scope of Biology

It examines several key aspects of life:

  • Structure of organisms
  • Classification of species
  • Function of living things
  • Growth and development
  • Origin of life
  • Evolution
  • Distribution of organisms

Are we included in the study of biology?

Yes, humans are absolutely included in the study of biology! As living organisms, we are part of the biological world and are studied in various branches including:

  • Human anatomy and physiology
  • Human genetics
  • Human evolution
  • Human ecology (our interactions with other species and the environment)

Components of Biodiversity

1. Genetic Diversity

Variation in genes within a species. This enables adaptation to environmental changes through survival and reproduction of individuals with advantageous genetic characteristics.

Example: Human genetic diversity is seen in traits like skin color, height, and disease resistance.

2. Species Diversity

The number and relative abundance of species in a given area. Includes both species richness (number of species) and species evenness (population distribution).

Example: A coral reef with many different species of fish, corals, and other marine life.

3. Ecosystem Diversity

The variety of different habitats, communities, and ecological processes. Includes both natural and managed ecosystems.

Example: The Amazon Rainforest with its various layers (forest floor, understory, canopy) supporting different species.

How They Are Connected

  • Genetic → Species: High genetic diversity helps species survive, increasing species diversity.
  • Species → Ecosystem: More species diversity contributes to stable ecosystem diversity.
  • Ecosystem → Genetic: Diverse ecosystems support more species, maintaining genetic variety.

Conservation of Biodiversity

In-situ Conservation

Conservation of species in their natural habitats

  • Protected areas
  • National parks
  • Wildlife sanctuaries
  • Biosphere reserves

Ex-situ Conservation

Conservation of species outside their natural habitats

  • Botanical gardens
  • Zoos
  • Seed banks
  • Gene banks

Conservation Actions

  • Habitat restoration
  • Species recovery programs
  • Sustainable resource management
  • Environmental education
  • Policy and legislation
  • Community involvement

Biodiversity Hotspots

Biodiversity hotspots are biogeographic regions with significant levels of biodiversity that are under threat from humans.

Criteria for Hotspots

  • Contains at least 1,500 species of endemic vascular plants
  • Has lost at least 70% of its original habitat

Philippines: A Biodiversity Hotspot

The Philippines is recognized as one of the world's 17 megadiverse countries, with:

  • Over 52,177 described species
  • 1,238 terrestrial vertebrate species (50% endemic)
  • 3,214 fish species (121 endemic)
  • 248 protected areas covering 7.79 million hectares
